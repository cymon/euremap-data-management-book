> "An inclusive construct that combines various movements and practices aiming to
make multilingual scientific knowledge openly available, accessible and reusable
for everyone, to increase scientific collaborations and sharing of information
for the benefits of science and society, and to open the processes of scientific
knowledge creation, evaluation and communication to societal actors beyond the
traditional scientific community"
>
>[UNESCO Open Science Recommendation 2021](https://unesdoc.unesco.org/ark:/48223/pf0000383323)

> "An approach to research based on open cooperative work that emphasizes the
sharing of knowledge, results and tools as early and widely as possible. It
operates on the principle of being ‘as open as possible, as closed as
necessary’"
>
>[EU Commission](https://rea.ec.europa.eu/open-science_en)


Open Science policies enhance the integrity of the scientfic process and aim to
make all data as open, interoperable, and resuable as possible.

Data management within the EUREMAP project follows directly from attempting to
implement the recommendations and policies of UNESCO and the EU Commission. This
includes publishing data according to [FAIR
principles](https://www.go-fair.org/fair-principles/) that data should be
Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Resusable.


**Here are a few key recommendations to keep in mind:**

- Create a Data Management Plan before beginning your project. The DMP is a
  evolving document and should be updated during the course of the project when
  deviations from the original plan are necessary.

- Deposit data sets in the most data-specific repository available and
  appropriate for your data type. For instance, DNA sequence data should be
  deposited in EMBL ENA rather than a general data repository such as Zenodo or
  Pangaea. Because EUREMAP is a Horizon Europe project of the European
  Commission we favour using data infrastructure that is part of the [European
  Open Science Cloud
  (EOSC)](https://research-and-innovation.ec.europa.eu/strategy/strategy-research-and-innovation/our-digital-future/open-science/european-open-science-cloud-eosc_en).
  
- Deposit data sets as soon after collection as is reasonably possible. Once you
  have collected and described your data (you have all the metadata), you should
  deposit it. This prinicple ensures that the data are not forgotten or lost,
  and they are deposited at the time when data collector is most conscious of
  their identity.

- If necessary, place an embargo on the date of release of the data set. This
  will not normally be necessary, but is an option. Most all repositories allow
  embargos on data release to be made, but often differ in whether the embargo
  is automatically or manually finished thereby publising the data.
